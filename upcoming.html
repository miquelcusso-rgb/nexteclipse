Next total solar eclipse · Spain & North Africa

☀️ Total Solar Eclipse — August 12, 2026

Wednesday, August 12, 2026 · Peak at 17:47 UTC

Greenland · Iceland · Northern Spain · Algeria · Tunisia · Libya

On August 12, 2026, the Moon's shadow will sweep across the North Atlantic and the Iberian Peninsula in one of the most anticipated astronomical events in decades. The path of totality — roughly 160 kilometres wide — will make landfall in Iceland, cross the Faroe Islands, and enter northern Spain from the northwest. For a narrow band of the Spanish meseta, the sky will go dark in the middle of the day for over two minutes.

The path crosses the provinces of Salamanca and Zamora, then continues south-east across Castile, touching the outskirts of Valladolid before heading towards the Mediterranean coast near Valencia. It then crosses the Balearic Islands and enters North Africa over Algeria, Tunisia and Libya, where it finally meets the horizon. Maximum totality — 2 minutes and 18 seconds — occurs over the Atlantic, but observers in Salamanca and Zamora will experience nearly the full duration.

2 min 18 sec max totality ~160 km path width 17:47 UTC maximum August · clear skies likely

Best viewing locations — Spain

The city of Salamanca sits almost perfectly inside the centre of the path — its Renaissance cathedral, the Plaza Mayor, and the Roman bridge over the Tormes River will all be plunged into a brief, eerie twilight. Zamora, 65 km north, is equally well placed. Outside the cities, the open tableland of the meseta offers unobstructed 360° horizons: ideal for photographing the corona. Observers in Valladolid will be just inside the northern edge of the path and will see the full totality if they move a few kilometres south. Cáceres and Mérida (Extremadura) are just south of the central path but will still experience over 1 minute 30 seconds of totality.

☀️ August in inland Spain means reliably clear skies. Book accommodation in Salamanca and Zamora well in advance — eclipse tourism will make hotels scarce months before.

The century's longest total solar eclipse · Egypt · Morocco · India

☀️ Total Solar Eclipse — August 2, 2027

Monday, August 2, 2027 · Peak at 10:07 UTC

Morocco · Algeria · Libya · Egypt · Saudi Arabia · Yemen · India

One year after the Spain eclipse, the longest total solar eclipse of the 21st century will darken a path from the western Sahara to the Indian subcontinent. On August 2, 2027, the Moon will be near perigee — its closest point to Earth — making its disc large enough to cover the Sun for an extraordinary 6 minutes and 23 seconds at maximum. This is the longest eclipse in over 100 years visible from land.

The path enters Africa near Agadir on Morocco's Atlantic coast, crosses the Atlas Mountains and the Grand Erg Oriental, and reaches maximum totality over Luxor and Aswan in southern Egypt. Every pharaonic temple on the Upper Nile — Karnak, Abu Simbel, the Valley of the Kings — will fall inside the zone of totality. The shadow then crosses the Red Sea, passes over Yemen and the Arabian Peninsula, and ends over the Bay of Bengal off the Indian coast.

6 min 23 sec max totality Luxor, Egypt near maximum Agadir, Morocco Atlantic entry Longest of the 21st century

Where to watch

Luxor, Egypt is the jewel location — surrounded by ancient temples, clear desert skies, and sitting within seconds of maximum totality. Aswan and Abu Simbel are equally spectacular. On the Atlantic side, Agadir and Marrakech offer easier access with direct flights from Europe, though Marrakech (further east) will be very close to the central path. Saudi Arabia's Empty Quarter will be largely inaccessible, but the coasts of Yemen and Oman offer extraordinary totality. In India, the shadow exits over Rajasthan and Gujarat.

⏱ At 6 minutes 23 seconds, observers will have time to watch the shadow bands ripple across the ground, observe two full Wilson-Devinney arcs, and even look for planets in the darkened sky. Don't miss this one.

Eclipse Types

☀️
Total Solar
The Moon completely covers the Sun. The corona becomes visible. Day turns to night along a narrow path (~150 km). Lasts seconds to 7+ minutes.
💍
Annular Solar
The Moon is near apogee, slightly too small to cover the Sun fully. A bright ring — the "ring of fire" — surrounds the lunar disc.
🌑
Total Lunar
Earth's shadow fully covers the Moon. The atmosphere refracts red light onto the lunar surface — the Blood Moon effect. Totality can last up to 1h 47min.
🌗
Partial Lunar
Only part of the Moon enters Earth's umbral shadow. The darkened portion appears noticeably different from the bright limb.

Source: NASA Goddard Space Flight Center · eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov
