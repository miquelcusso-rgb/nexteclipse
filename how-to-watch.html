Eclipse Guide

How to Watch the Solar Eclipse Safely

Eye protection, what to look for, and how to photograph the August 12, 2026 eclipse.

Solar Eye Safety — Golden Rules

Never look directly at the sun during a partial eclipse without certified eclipse glasses. Permanent retinal damage occurs in seconds — and is painless until hours later.
Use glasses certified ISO 12312-2 (also written EN ISO 12312-2). In the EU they must also carry the CE mark. Buy from astronomy clubs, planetariums, or reputable opticians — not street vendors.
Only during totality — when the Moon fully covers the Sun — is it safe to remove eclipse glasses. Replace them the instant the first sliver of the photosphere reappears.
Never use: ordinary sunglasses (even stacked), exposed film, CDs, smoked glass, or phone screens. None block the full spectrum of solar radiation.

Solar Eclipse Glasses

Certified Eclipse Glasses Safe
The only safe alternative to naked-eye viewing during totality is glasses specifically manufactured for solar observation. Look for the ISO 12312-2 certification printed on the frame — this international standard requires the lenses to block more than 99.997% of visible light and all harmful UV and infrared radiation.
In the European Union, eclipse glasses must also carry the CE mark. Be wary of counterfeits sold at inflated prices near eclipse events. A simple check: put the glasses on indoors and look at a bright lamp — you should see nothing except a faint, uniform disc. If you can see the room around you, the glasses are inadequate.
Never use: ordinary sunglasses (even stacked), exposed photographic film, CDs, smoked glass, or phone screens held up to the sun. None of these block the full spectrum of solar radiation.
Tip: Eclipse glasses sell out quickly as the date approaches. Buy early and keep them in a protective sleeve — a small scratch on the lens can make them unsafe.

DIY Projection Methods

Pinhole projection: stand with your back to the sun, pierce a 1 mm hole in one card, project onto the second. The further apart, the larger and dimmer the image.

Pinhole Projector DIY
A pinhole projector lets you watch the eclipse by projecting the sun's image onto a white surface — no glasses needed, and the whole family can watch the same image at once.
How to build it: Take two sheets of stiff white card. Pierce a small hole (~1 mm) in the centre of one sheet. Stand with your back to the sun, hold the pierced card above your shoulder, and project the image onto the second sheet in front of you. A distance of 50–70 cm gives a clear image.
Tip: A cardboard box with a pinhole at one end and a white interior makes an even better projector — the box blocks ambient light for a sharper image.
Natural Projectors DIY
Colander method: Hold a kitchen colander between the sun and a white surface — each hole projects a tiny crescent sun, creating a beautiful pattern on the pavement.
Tree shadows: The gaps between leaves act as natural pinhole projectors. As the eclipse advances, look at the ground beneath a leafy tree — hundreds of crescent suns will appear in the dappled shadows.
Telescope & Binoculars Filter required
A telescope or binoculars will reveal extraordinary detail — but they concentrate sunlight dramatically, making them dangerous without the correct filter.
Always fit the solar filter to the objective (front lens), never to the eyepiece. Use dedicated white-light solar filter film (Baader AstroSolar) cut to fit the front of the instrument.
Tip: Remove the solar filter the instant totality begins — then you can observe the corona through the eyepiece. Replace it the moment the diamond ring reappears.

During Totality — The Only Moment Without Glasses

Totality is the brief period when the Moon fully covers the solar disc. For observers inside the path on August 12, 2026 (up to 2 min 15 sec in Salamanca), this is the only time it is safe to look with the naked eye.

A total solar eclipse unfolds over ~2 hours — but the magic, totality, lasts only seconds to a few minutes.

C1 First contact. The Moon begins to move across the solar disc. A small notch appears on the sun's edge. Keep glasses on for the next ~90 minutes.
C2 Second contact. The Moon covers the last sliver of the photosphere. Remove glasses now — totality has begun. The corona appears.
Totality Observe with naked eyes. Look for the corona, prominences, Baily's beads, planets. In Spain: up to 2 min 15 sec maximum.
C3 Third contact. The diamond ring flares. Replace glasses immediately — the photosphere is reappearing. The partial phase begins again.
C4 Fourth contact. The Moon exits the solar disc. The eclipse is over.

Phenomena to Watch for During Totality

Diamond Ring
The last bright bead of sunlight through a lunar valley, surrounded by the glowing inner corona. Lasts only a second at C2 and C3.
Baily's Beads
Just before and after totality, sunlight shines through lunar valleys, creating a string of brilliant beads around the moon's edge.
Solar Corona
The outer atmosphere of the sun — a pearly white halo of plasma extending several solar radii. One of the most beautiful sights in astronomy.
Prominences
Bright pink-red flames of magnetised plasma looping off the chromosphere, visible as vivid arches at the lunar disc edge immediately after C2.
Shadow Bands
Just before totality, faint rippling bands of light and shadow sweep across pale surfaces. Lay a white sheet on the ground to see them.
Temperature Drop
Air temperature drops 2–5°C during totality. A perceptible wind develops. Birds fall silent, bees return to hives, and flowers begin to close.
Stars and Planets
The sky darkens to deep twilight. Venus will be visible near the eclipsed sun in 2026, and brighter stars appear briefly in the darkened sky.
360° Horizon Glow
The entire horizon glows with a 360° twilight ring — light from partial eclipse zones beyond the path. One of the most alien sights of a total solar eclipse.

Photography Guide

Smartphone
Attach a sheet of solar filter film over the lens before C2 and after C3. Use manual mode if available: ISO 100, maximum shutter speed. Tap to focus on the sun and lock exposure.
During totality: remove the filter and switch to auto. Point at the corona — the bright inner corona is easy to photograph. Use burst mode. Avoid digital zoom.
DSLR / Mirrorless
Before C2 and after C3: fit a dedicated solar filter (Baader AstroSolar ND 5.0). Settings: ISO 100, f/8, 1/500–1/2000s. Test on the partial phases.
During totality: remove the filter. Start at ISO 200, f/8, 1/500s for the inner corona. Bracket widely — the corona ranges over 10 stops of brightness. Use a cable release or timer.

A Final Note
Consider putting the camera down for part of totality and simply watching with your naked eyes. The experience of seeing the corona, the 360° twilight and the sudden chill is visceral and unrepeatable.
Many experienced eclipse chasers say their most vivid memories are from the eclipses they watched without a camera. Total solar eclipses visible from mainland Spain occur only once every few centuries.

Planning Your Eclipse Day

The August 12, 2026 eclipse is a late-afternoon event in Spain (local CEST time). Totality occurs with the sun at approximately 25–30° altitude — still well above the horizon, easy to observe, and ideal for wide-angle photography with landscape and totality in the same frame.
The best viewing locations for maximum totality duration in Spain are Salamanca and Zamora, both near the centreline of the path where totality exceeds 2 minutes. The open meseta landscape offers unobstructed views of the sky in all directions.
August in inland Castile and León has historically very low cloud cover — some of the most reliable clear-sky statistics in Western Europe. However, no weather forecast can be trusted more than a day or two in advance. Have a backup location plan.
Roads around Salamanca and Zamora will be extremely congested on August 12. Arrive the previous day. Mobile networks may be overwhelmed during totality — download offline maps and verify eclipse times before you leave home.
